What Is Yield? (Explained Like You're in 5th Grade)

Some tokens pay you just for holding them. That's yield — and it's the mechanism that makes RWA tokenization genuinely useful. Here's how it works, using the savings account your bank wishes you'd forget about.

In Part 03 we explained liquidity using parking lots and highways. This time we're going to explain yield — specifically, why certain tokens pay you just for holding them — using a savings account analogy that most banks wish didn't exist anymore.

The Savings Account That Actually Works

You probably have a savings account. You deposit money, the bank holds it, and in theory the bank pays you interest for the privilege of lending your money out to other people. That interest is yield — money your money earns while it sits there.

Here's the problem: most savings accounts today pay somewhere between 0.01% and 0.5% annually. On $1,000, that's $1 to $5 a year. Meanwhile, the US government has been paying 4–5% on short-term Treasury bills — the exact same bonds that banks buy with your deposits before paying you almost nothing back.

The gap between what you earn on a savings account and what the underlying asset actually yields is one of the oldest wealth transfers in banking. You're providing the capital. The bank captures most of the return.

The Web2 Analogy

A savings account is a middleman that takes your money, earns 4–5% on it, and pays you 0.01% while keeping the difference. A yield-bearing stablecoin cuts out the middleman and passes the Treasury yield directly to you. Same underlying asset. No bank in the middle.

What Yield Actually Is

Yield is the return generated by holding or deploying a financial asset. It comes in different forms:

Interest yield — the return from lending money. When you hold a Treasury bill, the US government pays you interest for lending it money for a defined period. When you deposit in a savings account, the bank pays you (a fraction of) the interest it earns on your behalf.

Rental yield — the income from an asset you own. A tokenized rental property distributes rental income to token holders. You own a fractional slice of the building; you receive a proportional slice of the rent.

Protocol yield — returns from participating in DeFi. When you provide liquidity to a decentralized exchange or lend assets through a protocol, you earn fees and interest from the activity your capital enables.

In traditional finance, most yield is captured by institutions and filtered down to retail investors after significant fees. In tokenized finance, yield flows directly to token holders via smart contract — automatically, proportionally, and without a human deciding whether to pass it on.

Yield-Bearing Stablecoins: The Product That Changes Everything

A stablecoin is a token designed to maintain a fixed value — usually $1. USDC and USDT are the most common. They don't yield anything. They're just digital dollars.

A yield-bearing stablecoin does something different: it holds a yield-generating asset in reserve — typically short-term US Treasuries — and passes the yield through to the token holder. The token stays worth roughly $1, but it grows slightly every day as interest accrues.

The RWA Version

Ondo Finance's USDY is a yield-bearing token backed by short-term US Treasuries. At current rates, it yields approximately 4–5% annually, accruing daily. You hold a token worth ~$1. Every day, that token earns a fraction of the Treasury rate. You don't do anything. The smart contract does the accounting. No bank captures the spread.

Why This Matters More Than It Sounds

Consider what yield-bearing stablecoins represent for someone outside the United States. A person in Argentina, Nigeria, or Vietnam historically had very limited options for protecting savings from local currency devaluation or inflation. US dollars were valuable but hard to access. US Treasuries — the safest, highest-quality yield in the world — were essentially inaccessible without a US brokerage account.

USDY can be held by anyone with a smartphone and a crypto wallet who meets the KYC requirements. Non-US residents currently have broader access than US residents due to regulatory constraints, which is itself a striking inversion: the assets backing US government debt are more accessible to people in Nairobi than in Nebraska.

That gap will close as regulation clarifies. But it illustrates the core point: yield-bearing stablecoins democratize access to returns that were previously institutional-only.

The Risk Picture — Honestly

Yield-bearing stablecoins are not savings accounts. They are not FDIC insured. They carry several risks that a traditional bank deposit doesn't.

Smart contract risk: The contract handling your yield could have a bug. Audited platforms reduce this risk, but don't eliminate it.

Counterparty risk: The issuer holds the underlying Treasuries through a custodian. If either the issuer or custodian fails, recovery depends on the legal structure — which varies significantly by platform.

Regulatory risk: The rules around yield-bearing stablecoins are still being written. The GENIUS Act provides a framework, but specifics for products like USDY are not fully settled.

For conservative capital — money you cannot afford to lose — these risks matter. For capital looking for yield above cash equivalents, yield-bearing stablecoins offer a genuinely new option that didn't exist five years ago.

"For the first time, a person anywhere in the world can hold a dollar-denominated token that earns the Treasury rate. The bank's job used to be the only way to do that. It isn't anymore."
