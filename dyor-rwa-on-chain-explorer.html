How to Read a Block Explorer: The On-Chain Verification Tool Most Investors Never Use

A block explorer is a search engine for a blockchain — every transaction, every wallet, every contract. Public, permanent, can't be faked. Here's how to use one to verify what an RWA project is actually doing.

In Part 01, we covered how to verify the people behind a project. This time we're going to cover how to verify what a project is actually doing — not through their marketing materials, not through Twitter, but directly from the blockchain itself.

A block explorer is a search engine for a blockchain. It lets you see every transaction ever recorded on that chain — every token transfer, every smart contract interaction, every wallet balance — without asking anyone's permission and without relying on anyone's claims. The data is there, it's public, and it doesn't lie.

Most crypto investors have never opened one. That is a research gap worth closing.

What a Block Explorer Actually Shows You

Every public blockchain has at least one explorer. Ethereum has Etherscan. Bitcoin has Mempool.space. Polygon has PolygonScan. Specific chains built for particular ecosystems have their own: the GROW token has its own native explorer at grow.elevatescan.com, where every on-chain transaction in the GROW ecosystem — token transfers, farming activity, supply chain records — is publicly visible and independently verifiable. Similarly, the AdventureWurld Chain Explorer shows every game match, reward distribution, and GULD transaction from the Kill The Boss tech test.

What all explorers have in common is this: they are the primary source. Not a spokesperson. Not a press release. The actual ledger.

One thing you'll need before you start: the token's contract address. This is a unique on-chain identifier — typically a string starting with 0x on EVM chains — that tells the explorer exactly which token you want to examine. You'll find it on the project's official site, on CoinMarketCap or CoinGecko under "Contract," or in their documentation. Once you have it, paste it into the explorer's search bar and you'll see every wallet holding that token, every transfer ever made, and the full contract code. Without the contract address, the explorer can show you the chain — but not the specific token you're researching.

What You Can Verify on Any Block Explorer
  • Token supply: How many tokens exist, how many are circulating, and whether the number matches what the project claims
  • Wallet distribution: Who holds the tokens and in what concentration — is 80% held by 3 wallets?
  • Transaction volume: Is the chain actually being used, or is the TVL number propped up by circular activity?
  • Smart contract code: Is the contract verified and readable? Unverified contracts are a red flag
  • Team wallet activity: Are founders selling? Are tokens being moved to exchanges?
  • Reserve wallets: Does the platform's reserve match what they claim? (e.g. a Conservator Wallet holding the game reward pool)

The Three Numbers to Check First

1. Token Concentration (The Whale Check)

Go to the token's page on its explorer and look at the list of top holders. A healthy token distribution has no single wallet holding more than 10–15% of supply outside of known locked contracts (team vesting, treasury, liquidity pools). What you're looking for:

If the top 5 wallets hold 70%+ of supply and none are labeled as exchange or contract addresses, you have a concentration risk. If one of those wallets starts moving tokens to a centralized exchange, that's a sell signal the project won't put in their newsletter.

Real Example

On grow.elevatescan.com, you can verify the GROW token's wallet distribution directly — who holds what, when transfers occurred, and whether the supply figures match the project's stated tokenomics. No trust required. The chain is the source of truth.

2. Transaction Velocity (The Activity Check)

A project claims 50,000 active users. The explorer shows 200 unique addresses transacting per day. That gap is not a rounding error — it's a claim that needs explaining.

Real activity leaves a pattern: diverse wallet addresses, varied transaction sizes, organic timing (not suspiciously even intervals that suggest automated bot activity), and transaction types that match the product's described use cases.

Bot-inflated activity looks different: a small number of addresses transacting in tight loops, suspiciously round amounts, and transaction patterns that cluster around announcement dates before going quiet.

3. Contract Verification (The Code Check)

On Etherscan and most EVM explorers, you can view whether a smart contract's source code has been verified and published. A verified contract means the code is publicly readable. You — or any developer — can see exactly what the contract does before trusting it with funds.

An unverified contract is a contract you cannot audit. It's a black box. For any RWA project where you're trusting a smart contract to hold value, distribute yield, or execute legal rights — unverified code is a disqualifying red flag unless the project provides a compelling explanation.

Reading a Specific Transaction

Every transaction on a blockchain has a hash — a unique identifier that looks like this: 0x4a7f...b92c. You can paste any transaction hash into the relevant explorer and see exactly what happened: which wallets were involved, what tokens moved, how much gas was paid, and what smart contract functions were called.

This is how you verify specific claims. A project says "we distributed $2.3 million in rewards to farmers last quarter." Find the transaction hashes. Paste them into the explorer. Verify the amounts, the receiving wallets, and the dates. Either the on-chain record confirms the claim or it doesn't.

The Analogy

A block explorer is like a publicly accessible bank statement for every wallet on the network. You can see every deposit, withdrawal, and transfer — but only the outgoing and incoming amounts, not the identity behind the wallet. The record is permanent, public, and cannot be altered. When a project makes a financial claim, this is where you verify it.

What You Cannot Verify On-Chain

The blockchain verifies what happened on-chain. It doesn't verify what happened in the physical world.

A token representing a gold bar is only as real as the gold in the vault. A token representing a carbon credit is only as real as the underlying project's emissions reductions. A token representing a farm's supply chain is only as trustworthy as the people entering data at the farm level.

On-chain verification is a necessary condition for RWA due diligence. It is not sufficient. The blockchain confirms that tokens exist, that they moved, and that contracts executed as written. It cannot confirm that the real-world assets those tokens represent actually exist in the form claimed.

This is why the research stack combines on-chain verification with the LinkedIn team verification from Part 01, legal structure review, and the red flags checklist coming in Part 05. No single tool covers everything — each covers what the others can't.

Where to Start

Explorer Quick Reference
  • Ethereum / ERC-20 tokens: etherscan.io
  • Polygon (MATIC chain): polygonscan.com
  • Solana: solscan.io
  • BNB Chain: bscscan.com
  • GROW token (native): grow.elevatescan.com
  • AdventureWurld Chain: adventurewurld.elevatescan.com
  • Any chain: Search "[chain name] block explorer" — every serious chain has one
  • Finding a specific token: You'll need the token's contract address — a unique identifier that looks like 0x1234...abcd. Find it on the project's official website, their CoinMarketCap or CoinGecko page, or their documentation. Paste it into the explorer's search bar to see all holders, transfers, and activity for that specific token. Never use a contract address from an unofficial source — fake tokens with similar names are a common scam.
