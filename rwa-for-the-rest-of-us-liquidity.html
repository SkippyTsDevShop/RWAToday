What Is Liquidity? (Explained Like You're in 5th Grade)

Liquidity is the difference between a parking lot and a highway. Here's what it means for RWA tokenization — and the one question to ask before you invest in any tokenized asset.

In Part 01 we explained tokenization using Spotify. In Part 02 we explained smart contracts using a vending machine. This time we're going to explain liquidity — one of the most important concepts in all of finance — using a parking lot.

Specifically: the difference between a parking lot and a highway.

The Parking Lot Problem

Imagine you own a single parking spot in a busy city. It's a real asset. It has real value. Someone would pay good money for it. But if you decided you needed cash tomorrow morning, your ability to convert that parking spot into money is genuinely difficult. You'd need to find a buyer, agree on a price, hire a lawyer to transfer the deed, wait for the transaction to close — likely several weeks at minimum, possibly months.

During that entire window, your money is stuck. The asset is parked.

Now imagine you own a share of Apple stock. If you decide you need cash tomorrow morning, you open your brokerage app, hit sell, and have cash in your account within two days. Millions of buyers are on the other side of that trade at any given moment. The price is visible and fair. The transaction is frictionless.

That difference — the ease and speed with which an asset can be converted to cash without meaningfully affecting its price — is liquidity.

The Web2 Analogy

A parking spot is illiquid: hard to sell, slow to settle, no visible price. A stock is liquid: instant to sell, millions of buyers, price visible in real time. Liquidity is the highway that turns parking lots into markets. The more buyers and sellers, the faster and cheaper it is to convert your asset into cash when you need it.

Why Most Real-World Assets Are Parking Lots

The vast majority of valuable things in the world are parking lots. Real estate. Private company equity. Fine art. Farmland. Infrastructure. Business loans. These asset classes hold trillions of dollars of value — but converting them to cash requires finding a specific buyer, negotiating, completing legal transfers, and waiting.

This illiquidity isn't just inconvenient. It shapes who can own these assets in the first place. If you need your money back on short notice, you can't afford to lock it into something illiquid. That effectively excludes most retail investors from the most consistently high-performing asset classes in history. Private credit. Private equity. Commercial real estate. The assets that institutional investors have used to generate outsized returns for decades are, by design, inaccessible to anyone who might need their money back.

What Tokenization Does to Liquidity

Tokenizing an asset doesn't automatically make it liquid. This is an important caveat that gets glossed over in most RWA coverage. A token representing a fraction of a building is only liquid if someone else wants to buy it. The token is the format. The market is the liquidity.

What tokenization does is make liquidity possible in a way it wasn't before. It removes the structural barriers — the legal friction, the minimum transaction size, the settlement delays — that prevented secondary markets from forming around illiquid assets.

The RWA Version

Lofty tokenizes US rental properties on Algorand. Each token represents a fractional ownership share. The platform runs a secondary market where token holders can list their shares for sale. It doesn't have the depth of the NYSE — but it exists, it settles on-chain in minutes, and it allows investors to exit positions without waiting for the property itself to sell. That's a parking lot that got a highway on-ramp.

The Liquidity Spectrum

Not all RWA tokens are equally liquid. Where an asset sits on the liquidity spectrum matters enormously for how you should think about holding it.

RWA Liquidity Spectrum — From Highway to Parking Lot
  • Tokenized Treasuries (USDY, OUSG) — Most liquid. Redeemable through the issuer on short windows. Deep institutional demand. Closest to cash.
  • Tokenized Gold (PAXG, XAUT) — Highly liquid. Trades on major exchanges 24/7. Price tracks spot gold. Sell anytime.
  • Tokenized Real Estate (Lofty, RealT) — Moderate liquidity. Secondary market exists but thin. Don't count on a fast exit.
  • Tokenized Private Credit — Low liquidity. Lock-up periods common. Designed as hold-to-maturity. Exit before maturity may not be possible.
  • Tokenized Private Equity — Very low liquidity. Earliest stage of tokenization. Secondary markets barely exist yet.

Why Liquidity Is the Whole Game

Here's the core insight: every financial innovation in history that created widespread wealth did so by making previously illiquid things liquid. The joint-stock company made private business equity transferable. The mortgage-backed security made real estate loans tradeable. The ETF made diversified fund exposure instantly liquid.

Each of those innovations came with risks — sometimes catastrophic ones. Liquidity isn't inherently safe. Creating a market for something means that market can crash. The 2008 financial crisis was partly a liquidity crisis: assets that looked liquid turned out not to be when everyone tried to sell at once.

Tokenization is the next iteration of this pattern. It is creating liquid markets for assets that have never had them. The opportunity is real. So is the risk that liquidity can be illusory — thin order books, correlated sellers, and smart contract vulnerabilities can make an asset look tradeable right up until the moment it isn't.

"A tokenized asset is only as liquid as the market that exists for it. The token is the parking spot. The secondary market is the highway. You need both."

The Practical Takeaway

When you evaluate any tokenized RWA, ask one question before anything else: what is the exit? Not theoretically — specifically. Who buys this token from me if I need cash in 30 days? Is there a redemption window through the issuer? Is there a secondary market with real volume? Is there a lock-up period I'm agreeing to?

The best tokenized assets answer this question clearly. The ones that don't — or that point to a secondary market that doesn't yet exist — are parking lots calling themselves highways.
